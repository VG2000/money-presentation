Part One

What Is Money?

Let's start with the fundamental question: what actually is money in the modern UK economy?

Most Money Is Bank Deposits

97%
of money in circulation
is bank deposits

Bank of England, December 2013

When we talk about "money" in the economy, we're overwhelmingly talking about numbers in bank accounts ‚Äî not physical cash.

The composition of UK broad money:

Bank deposits (commercial bank money): 97%

Currency (notes and coins): 3%

This means understanding money creation requires understanding how these bank deposits come into existence.

Source: Bank of England (2014) "Money creation in the modern economy", Quarterly Bulletin Q1

The first key insight: 97% of money in the UK economy is not physical cash, but numbers in bank accounts ‚Äî commercial bank deposits. Only 3% is actual currency. So understanding money creation means understanding how these deposits are created.

Why the ‚Äú97%‚Äù Figure Is Misunderstood

Fact: 97% of the money we hold is commercial bank deposits.

But this is a stock measure ‚Äî it tells us what type of money people hold today, not how that money came into existence.

Commercial Bank Lending

When banks make loans, they create matching deposits. This is a major source of money during economic expansions.

Government Spending

Every payment from HM Treasury increases deposits in the commercial banking system. Government deficits are therefore a second source of money creation.

Key insight: ‚Äú97%‚Äù describes the form money takes (bank deposits), not the origin of those deposits. Both private lending and government spending expand the money supply.

No chart is shown here because neither the Bank of England, ONS, nor HM Treasury publish a dataset that splits money creation between commercial lending and government spending. Monetary statistics record bank lending as increases in broad money, while government spending is recorded as expenditure rather than ‚Äúmoney creation,‚Äù even though it injects deposits and reserves into the system. As a result, there is no official, directly comparable series for the two flows.

There are two distinct types of money. Central bank money includes physical currency and bank reserves held at the Bank of England. Commercial bank money is the deposits in our bank accounts ‚Äî IOUs from banks to us. The crucial point is that deposits are created by commercial banks, not by the central bank. Bank deposits are a promise to deliver central bank money (either cash or reserves via bank transfer)

Money exists in a hierarchy. At the top is Parliament with the power to tax. Below that is the Bank of England issuing reserves and currency. Then commercial banks creating deposits. At the bottom are money users. Each layer's money can be converted into money from the layer above ‚Äî deposits can be exchanged for cash, for instance.

The common misconception is that banks are intermediaries ‚Äî collecting deposits from savers and lending them out. But the Bank of England explicitly states this is wrong. Saving doesn't increase funds available for lending; it just shifts deposits from one place to another.

The Reality

Loans Create Deposits

When a bank makes a loan, it simultaneously creates a matching deposit:

"Whenever a bank makes a loan, it simultaneously creates a matching deposit in the borrower's bank account, thereby creating new money." ‚Äî Bank of England (2014)

This is sometimes called "fountain pen money" ‚Äî created at the stroke of a banker's pen.

Here's how it actually works. When a bank makes a loan, it doesn't transfer existing money ‚Äî it creates new money. The bank adds a loan asset to its balance sheet and simultaneously creates a matching deposit liability. The borrower gets a new deposit asset and a loan liability. New money has been created from nothing.

"While the money multiplier theory can be a useful way of introducing money and banking in economic textbooks, it is not an accurate description of how money is created in reality." ‚Äî Bank of England (2014)

Reserves don't constrain lending

Banks decide how much to lend based on profitable opportunities. They obtain reserves afterwards.

No binding reserve requirements

The UK has no reserve ratio requirement. The BoE supplies reserves on demand to meet settlement needs.

Central banks set interest rates, not the quantity of money. Reserves follow lending, not the other way around.

The textbook "money multiplier" theory is also wrong. It claims central banks control money by setting reserve quantities that banks "multiply up." But as the Bank of England states, this is not how it works. Banks lend first based on profitable opportunities, then obtain reserves. The UK has no reserve ratio requirement. Central banks control interest rates, not money quantities.

What Actually Constrains Bank Lending?

Banks can't create unlimited money. Three sets of constraints limit money creation:

1

Market Forces

‚Ä¢ Profitability in competitive market

‚Ä¢ Capital constraints and risk assessment

‚Ä¢ Liquidity and funding management after lending (not a prerequisite to lend)

2

Borrower Behaviour

‚Ä¢ Willingness to borrow

‚Ä¢ Loan repayments destroy money

‚Ä¢ "Reflux" of unwanted money

3

Monetary Policy

‚Ä¢ Interest rates affect loan demand

‚Ä¢ Prudential regulation (capital, liquidity)

‚Ä¢ Ultimate constraint

The key point: Monetary policy is the ultimate constraint. The BoE influences lending through interest rates, not by controlling reserves.

Source: Bank of England (2014) ‚Äî see also Farag, Harland and Nixon (2013) on capital and liquidity requirements

So what does constrain bank lending if not reserves? Three things: market forces including profitability and risk; borrower behaviour including demand for loans and repayments that destroy money; and most importantly, monetary policy ‚Äî interest rates set by the central bank, plus prudential regulation on capital and liquidity.

Summary: Commercial Bank Money Creation

‚úì
Banks create money when they lend. They don't lend out pre-existing deposits ‚Äî the loan creates the deposit simultaneously.
‚úì
The money multiplier is a myth. Banks decide how much to lend first, then obtain reserves. Central banks set interest rates, not the quantity of money.
‚úì
Loan repayment destroys money. Just as lending creates deposits, repayment extinguishes them.

"Most of the money in circulation is created, not by the printing presses of the Bank of England, but by the commercial banks themselves."

‚Äî Bank of England (2014)

But what about government spending? How does the state create and inject money into the economy?

To summarise Part 2: banks create money through lending, not by intermediating deposits. The money multiplier doesn't describe reality. And loan repayment destroys money just as lending creates it. Now let's turn to how the government creates money.

The UK's institutional architecture

Now we turn to the less understood topic: how the UK government actually creates money when it spends.

The Consolidated Fund

The core legal and accounting structure at the heart of UK public finance

Established 1787

"One fund into which shall flow every stream of public revenue, and from which shall come the supply for every service"

‚Äî HM Treasury (2024)

What it does:

‚Ä¢ Origin of all departmental expenditures

‚Ä¢ Source of government securities issuance

‚Ä¢ Destination for most government revenue

Key insight: The Consolidated Fund acts as a sovereign line of credit that only Parliament can authorize drawing upon.

The Central Funds

Consolidated Fund

‚ÄúCurrent account‚Äù

National Loans Fund

Lending & borrowing

Contingencies Fund

Urgent expenditure

Governed by Exchequer and Audit Departments Act 1866, National Loans Act 1968

The Consolidated Fund is the heart of UK public finance, established in 1787. It's the origin of all government spending and destination of most revenue. Crucially, it functions as a sovereign line of credit that only Parliament can authorize. Several related funds work together, including the National Loans Fund for borrowing operations.

The UK Government's Financial Architecture

This diagram shows the institutional architecture. At the top is HM Treasury with its central funds and departmental accounts in the Government Banking Service. These connect to accounts at the Bank of England. Below that are commercial banks with their reserve accounts, connected to the public who hold deposits. All government transactions flow through this structure.

"This enactment shall not be construed to empower the Treasury or any authority to direct the payment... of expenditure not sanctioned by any Act whereby services are or may be charged on the Consolidated Fund, or by a vote of the House of Commons..."

Key point: Individual departments cannot spend without Parliamentary authorization. The Comptroller and Auditor General verifies each requisition before granting Treasury a "credit" on the Consolidated Fund.

Two Supply and Appropriation Acts typically passed each year (July and March)

All spending must be authorized by Parliament. There are two types: Standing Services permanently authorized by specific Acts, and Supply Services voted annually. The Comptroller and Auditor General must verify each requisition before Treasury gets credit on the Consolidated Fund. No department can spend without Parliamentary sanction.

The Government Spending Mechanism

1
Parliamentary Authorization ‚Äî Parliament passes legislation authorizing expenditure from the Consolidated Fund
2
Treasury Requisition ‚Äî HM Treasury raises a monthly requisition to the Comptroller and Auditor General
3
Credit Granted ‚Äî The Comptroller grants Treasury a "credit" on the Consolidated Fund account at the Bank of England
4
Money Created ‚Äî Bank of England debits CF account, credits public deposits ‚Üí these become bank reserves when spent into private sector
5
Payment Settled ‚Äî Government departments pay recipients; commercial bank deposits increase
Critical insight: The allocation of spending within Government Banking Service is conducted entirely by balance sheet expansion and is contingent only upon the authority of Parliament. No pre-existing funds are required.

Source: Berkeley et al. (2025)

Here's the step-by-step spending mechanism. Parliament authorizes, Treasury requisitions, the Comptroller grants credit, then the Bank of England debits the Consolidated Fund and credits government accounts. When spent to the private sector, this becomes commercial bank reserves and deposits. The key insight: this is all balance sheet expansion. No pre-existing funds are required.

The "Growing Produce of the Fund"

The Consolidated Fund account at the Bank of England starts every day with a zero balance, yet orders for issues are nevertheless made and fulfilled.

Finance Act 1954, Section 34(3)

"Any sum charged by any Act, whenever passed, on the Consolidated Fund shall be charged also on the growing produce of the Fund"

This connects current spending to "all the revenues to be received in the future" ‚Äî framing expenditure as credit advanced on the security of future tax revenues.

What This Means

All spending arises as new money advanced as credit, not from taxation or borrowing.

No "Intertemporal Budget Constraint"

There is no ex-ante limit on current or future spending from the accounting mechanics.

Conclusion: It is the expenditure that causes a matching future tax liability ‚Äî not the other way around. The government's taxing power provides the creditworthiness, not the funding.

See Brittain (1959) "The British Budgetary System" for historical context

This is the key revelation. The Consolidated Fund starts each day at zero, yet spending still occurs. The Finance Act 1954 refers to the "growing produce of the Fund" ‚Äî connecting spending to future tax revenues. Spending is credit advanced against future taxes, not spending of pre-collected taxes. The government's power to tax provides creditworthiness, not funding.

Taxes are finally settled using Bank of England money (reserves), not commercial bank deposits.

The "Exchequer Sweep": At end of each day, all public deposit accounts are swept to zero, with net balances consolidated into the National Loans Fund. Spending and revenue are only reconciled at day's end.
Taxation is the reverse process. When someone pays tax, their deposit decreases, their bank's reserves decrease, HMRC's account is credited, and ultimately the Consolidated Fund is credited. Taxes destroy money ‚Äî the reverse of spending creating it. At day's end, the "Exchequer Sweep" consolidates all balances.

Debt Issuance: What It's Actually For

Policy Choice

The "Full Funding Rule"

"An overarching requirement of debt management policy is that the government fully finances its projected financing requirement each year through the sale of debt."

‚Äî DMO Annual Report 2023

Historical Purpose (pre-2009)

Under the "corridor" system, debt issuance drained excess reserves to maintain the target interest rate.

‚Ä¢ Net spending adds reserves

‚Ä¢ Bond sales drain reserves

‚Ä¢ Keeps interbank rate on target

Current Purpose (post-QE)

Under the "floor" system with excess reserves, debt issuance primarily serves different functions:

‚Ä¢ Provides safe store of value

‚Ä¢ Supplies secure collateral for repo markets

‚Ä¢ Supports financial sector liquidity needs

The paradox: QE removes gilts from private sector balance sheets, while the full funding rule requires continued issuance. These policies work at cross-purposes.
The Ways and Means Account: Treasury's overdraft facility at the BoE remains available ‚Äî used in crises (e.g., April 2020). Gilt issuance is a policy choice, not an operational necessity.

Since 2006, the BoE pays interest on reserves, making the quantity of reserves less relevant for interest rate control.

The "full funding rule" requires government to issue debt matching its borrowing requirement. Historically, it drained reserves to control interest rates. Today, with excess reserves and the BoE paying interest on them, debt issuance mainly provides safe assets and collateral to the financial sector. The Ways and Means overdraft at the BoE remains available ‚Äî gilt issuance is a policy choice.

Part Five

Sectoral Balances

The accounting identity that connects all sectors

Now we'll examine sectoral balances ‚Äî a powerful accounting framework that shows how government, private sector, and foreign sector financial positions must sum to zero.

What Are Sectoral Balances?

Government Balance + Private Sector Balance + Foreign Balance = 0

Sectoral balances are an accounting identity derived from national accounts. Every pound spent by one sector is received by another ‚Äî the flows must balance.

Government Sector

Deficit = spending > taxation

Surplus = taxation > spending

Private Sector

Surplus = saving > investment

Deficit = investment > saving

Foreign Sector

Surplus = imports > exports (from UK perspective)

Deficit = exports > imports

Key Insight: If the government runs a surplus, by accounting identity, the non-government sectors (private + foreign) must run a deficit of equal size.
Why this matters: A government deficit is not "borrowing from the future" ‚Äî it is the source of private sector net financial assets. Government deficits = non-government surpluses.

Based on Godley, W. (1999) "Seven Unsustainable Processes" and national accounting identities (GDP = C + I + G + NX)

Sectoral Balances: United Kingdom

Use ‚Üì to see other countries

This chart shows UK sectoral balances over time. Notice how when the government deficit increases, the private sector surplus tends to increase as well. The three balances always sum to zero.

Sectoral Balances: Japan

Large government deficits matched by large private surpluses

Japan has run large government deficits for decades, which have been matched by large private sector surpluses. This explains how Japan maintains low interest rates despite high debt-to-GDP ratios.

Sectoral Balances: United States

Government deficits fund both private surpluses and current account deficits

The US typically runs both government and current account deficits, meaning the private domestic sector can still accumulate net financial assets despite the trade deficit.

Sectoral Balances: Germany

Export surpluses allow lower government deficits

Germany's large trade surplus means the government can run balanced budgets or surpluses while the private sector still saves. This is only possible because other countries run deficits.

Sectoral Balances: France

Constrained by Eurozone fiscal rules?

France, as a Eurozone member, faces constraints that currency-issuing nations don't. The sectoral balances still hold, but policy options are more limited.

What Sectoral Balances Tell Us

The "Balanced Budget" Problem

If the government balances its budget and the country runs a trade deficit, the private sector must go into deficit (spend more than income).

This is unsustainable ‚Äî private debt accumulates until a crisis forces correction.

Historical Pattern

Every major financial crisis has been preceded by a period where government surpluses forced the private sector into deficit.

Government Deficits Enable Private Saving

For the private sector to net save (accumulate financial assets), the government must run a deficit of equal size (adjusted for trade). This is completely normal, the UK has run deficits for for 95% of the time since WWII.

Policy Implication

Arbitrary deficit targets ignore sectoral balance realities. The "appropriate" deficit depends on private sector saving desires and the trade balance ‚Äî not a fixed percentage of GDP.

The key question changes: Instead of "how do we reduce the deficit?", ask "what deficit is consistent with full employment and private sector financial stability?"

See Godley's analysis of the "Goldilocks economy" in the late 1990s predicting the 2001 recession and housing bubble

The policy implications are profound. If we target arbitrary deficit reduction while running trade deficits, we force the private sector into deficit. This creates unsustainable private debt growth that eventually triggers financial crises. The right question is what deficit is consistent with desired private sector outcomes.

Part Six

Implications

Real constraints vs perceived constraints

Finally, let's consider what all this means for policy. What are the real constraints on government spending?

What These Findings Mean

Constraints That Are NOT Valid

Liquidity Risk

No circumstance where government has "insufficient money" for expenditure requirements

Market Risk / Bond Vigilantes

Banks purchase securities to exchange excess reserves for higher-yielding assets. DMO faces a "seller's market"

Default Risk

Debt payments are Standing Services ‚Äî permanently authorized by Parliament. Default requires explicit repeal

The REAL Constraints

Inflation

Spending where resources are scarce can create inflationary pressure

Resource Availability

Physical resources, labour, materials are the true limits ‚Äî not accounting entries

Political/Democratic Choice

Parliament decides priorities. Fiscal rules are policy choices, not operational necessities

The core insight: The question is not "can we afford it?" but rather "do we have the real resources?" and "what are the inflationary implications?"

"The Government's banking arrangements... ensure that all expenditure authorized by Parliament can be settled."

‚Äî HM Treasury, FOI response 2020

What isn't a valid constraint: liquidity risk, market risk from "bond vigilantes", or default risk‚Ä¶

Central Bank "Independence"

The Bank of England is never independent in the sense that it can refuse to facilitate public expenditure.

1

Legal Obligation

Under the 1866 Act, the BoE has no discretion over whether to extend credit to the Consolidated Fund. This was not changed by the Bank of England Act 1998.

2

System Dependence

The BoE's assets are almost entirely government securities or loans collateralized by them. HM Treasury provides capital, indemnities, and deposit insurance backing.

What "Independence" Actually Means

Operational independence for monetary policy (setting interest rates) ‚Äî not independence from facilitating Parliamentary-authorized spending.

The Hierarchy

Parliament's power to tax provides supreme creditworthiness. The BoE is a wholly owned subsidiary of HM Treasury, subject to its direction.

Conclusion: Consolidating the Treasury and central bank balance sheets for analytical purposes reflects institutional reality in the UK. The government self-finances; the BoE executes.

See Financial Services and Markets Act 2000; Banking Act 2009 for Treasury backstop powers

Central bank "independence" doesn't mean the BoE can refuse to execute Parliamentary spending. What does independence means? One way to frame this is that the Bank, being independent, is able to cost the government recently ¬£100B a year in interest payments (which are solely due to rate targeting monetary policy), but this cost is never scrutinised like all other departments. Only 3 other departments have larger costs (NHS, DWP and Education).

Summary: Key Takeaways

1
Commercial banks create money through lending ‚Äî not by lending out deposits.
2
Government spending is money creation. The Consolidated Fund provides sovereign credit backed by Parliament's taxing power.
3
Taxation destroys money rather than funding expenditure. Taxes are settled after spending occurs.
4
Debt issuance is a policy choice, not an operational necessity. Today, it mainly provides safe assets and collateral.
5
Real constraints are inflation and resources ‚Äî not "affordability" or liquidity. The question changes from "can we afford it?" to "do we have the capacity?"
To summarise: Banks create 97% of money through lending. Government spending is money creation via the Consolidated Fund. Taxation destroys money rather than funding spending. Debt issuance is a policy choice, not necessity. And the real constraints are inflation and resource availability ‚Äî not affordability.
